Meridian Supply Co. maintains a structured approach to managing excess and non-performing inventory in order to minimize financial loss and optimize warehouse capacity. Products classified as dead stock may be considered eligible for return to suppliers if they meet specific conditions, including being in resalable condition, within original packaging, and falling within the agreed return window outlined in supplier contracts, typically ranging from 60 to 180 days from receipt. Items approved for return are subject to restocking fees, which generally range between 10% and 25% depending on the supplier agreement, product category, and condition of goods. Any exceptions must be negotiated directly with the supplier and documented through the procurement team.

Inventory performance is continuously monitored using days-of-stock metrics to identify slow-moving items early. Products exceeding 30 days of stock are flagged for initial review, while those surpassing 60 days are escalated for action planning. Items reaching 90 days of stock are formally classified as slow-moving and require intervention, which may include pricing adjustments, promotional bundling, or supplier discussions. This tiered monitoring approach ensures that inventory risks are identified proactively before transitioning into dead stock.

Price markdowns are used strategically to accelerate inventory turnover and must follow a controlled authorization process. Department managers are authorized to approve markdowns up to 15% for items identified as slow-moving. Reductions between 16% and 30% require approval from the inventory control director, while any markdown exceeding 30% must be reviewed and approved by senior leadership, typically at the director or executive level. All markdown decisions must be supported by sales data, inventory aging reports, and margin impact analysis to ensure financial accountability.

For items that remain slow-moving despite pricing adjustments, Meridian Supply Co. engages in consignment swap arrangements with select suppliers. Under this policy, qualifying inventory may be exchanged for higher-demand products, provided the supplier relationship supports flexible inventory agreements. These swaps are typically negotiated by the procurement and vendor management teams and require that returned goods meet quality and condition standards. The goal of this approach is to rebalance inventory without incurring additional procurement costs while maintaining strong supplier partnerships.

In cases where inventory cannot be returned, exchanged, or effectively sold through markdowns, the company follows a structured disposal process. Depending on the product type and condition, items may be donated to charitable organizations, sold through liquidation channels at significantly reduced prices, or written off entirely as a financial loss. All disposal actions must be documented and approved by finance to ensure proper accounting treatment and compliance. This final step ensures that obsolete inventory does not continue to occupy valuable storage space or distort inventory valuation.
